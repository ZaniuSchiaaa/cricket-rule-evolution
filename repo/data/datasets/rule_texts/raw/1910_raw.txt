PREFACE.
The aim of this publication is to remove difficulties which
are known to exist, although they are not always apparent.
Hundreds of cases arc sent to the M.C.C. for decision every
year. It is from this source that the chief difficulties have
become manifest. Saturday and League matches arc especially
productive of disputes and it is hoped that those who read
these notes may find an answer to any doubt which may arise
as to the proper interpretation of the Laws of Cricket.
F. E. LACEY, Secretary, M.C.C.
Lord’s Cricket Ground,
St. John’s Wood, N.W.
May, 1910.

Notes on Law 11.
A bowler may bowl with both feet behind the bowling urease provided
that one foot is within the return crease.
If a bowler knock the wicket with is foot in delivering the ball ami remove
a bail, snub act does not in itself justify an Umpire in calling “ No Ball.”
When part, of the bowler’s foot is grounded on or outside the return
crease the Umpire should call “ No Ball.”
Many bowlers deliver the ball with the toe of the back foot pointing
towards the near wicket, and with the heel towards and over the return
crease. If the bowler so delivers the ball with the heel on the ground, the
Umpire “No Balls” him (the foot not being entirely within the return
crease). If he delivers with the heel raised oti the ground and keeps that
part of the foot which is on the ground within the return cicase the delivery
is allowed as fair.
One foot must be grounded behind the bowling and within the return
crease when the ball is delivered.
12.—If the Bowler shall bowl the Ball so high over or so wide of the
wicket, that, in the opinion of the Umpire, it is not within reach of the
Striker, the Umpire shall call “Wide Ball.”
Notes on Law 12.
The test for the Umpire to apply in deciding if the ball is wide is
whether it is within reach of the Striker.
When the ball has been delivered but remains stationary before reaching
the striker no inns can be added to the score, unless it has been struck by
the striker. The striker has a right to hit the ball without interference by
the field. The Umpire must decide if the ball has been “delivered.”
If the striker hit a ball which has been called “Wide” the call should
be ignored.
When only one run has been made from a “Wide Ball” the batsmen
do not cross over to the {jositions they were in before the run was made.
When a wide is called the ball does not on that account become “dead”
and the batsman may be out stumped or run out.
13.—The Ball shall be bowled in Overs of six balls from each wicket
alternately. When six balls have been bowled, and the ball is finally settled
in the Bowler’s or Wicket-keeper’s hands, the Umpire shall call “Over.”
Neither a “No Ball ” nor “Wide Ball” shall be reckoned as one of the
“Over.”
Notes on Law 13.
This Law should be read with Law 16. Though provision is made in
Law 16 by which the Striker may obtain runs from a “No Ball” the fact
remains that “neither a No Ball nor Wide Ball shall be reckoned as one of
the over.”
Although not counted in the over No Balls and Wides are recorded in
their proper place on the score sheet.
An over should always be finished, unless a wicket fall within two minutes
of time (see Law 45), or unless there is a local rule extending the time.
It is Umpire’s and not the Scorer’s duty to call over.

When a batsman has retired owing to illness, injury, or some other

THE BOUNDARY.
(See Notes to Law 2.)
If a ball hit or pass over or through the boundary or is carried over it
by the fieldsman the Umpire should call a boundary hit, but a fieldsman
may lean against the boundary to catch or field a ball, and if a fieldsman
when standing within the playing ground, even though he be touching the
boundary, catch a ball outside the boundary the batsman should be given
out.
When the ball touches the boundary it becomes “Dead.”
The Umpire is not a boundary.
It is for the Umpire to decide if a hit has reached the boundary.
If a seat or other obstacle is placed in the playing ground, within
boundaries, and such obstacle is struck by the ball it cannot be regarded
as a boundary unless so arranged by the Umpires.

INSTRUCTIONS TO UMPIRES.
FITNESS OF GROUND.
Law 43.—At the commencement of a Match, the Umpires may be
appealed to by either side as to the fitness of the Ground for play.
Should they not agree, play will not commence until they are agreed.
In case of interruption from min, as soon as the rain has ceased, the
Umpires shall, immediately, without further instniction, inspect the wicket,
unaccompanied by any of the players, and decide upon its fitness. Should
it prove unfit, they shall continue to inspect at intervals, until they decide
that it is fit for play, when they shall call upon the players to resume
the game.
The ground is unfit for play—when water stands on the surface, or
when it is so wet, muddy, or slippery as to deprive the bowlers of a
reasonable foothold, or the fieldsmen of the power of free movement.
The Umpires arc not to be biassed by the opinions of cither side, still
less are they to allow themselves to be influenced by the impatience of the
spectators for a resumption of the game, and arc not to be induced, by the
public interest in a particular match, to declare the ground fit for play,
unless they would consider that Ground fit under any circumstances.
FITNESS OF LIGHT FOR PLAY.
The Umpires may decide, on appeal from the Captains, should the latter
disagree, that there is not sufficient light for Play. Should the light
improve before the time for drawing stumps, they shall, without waiting
for instructions, call upon the players to resume the game.
In the event of the Captains agreeing as to the condition of the ground
or light-, the Umpires will, so far, be relieved of their responsibility.
19
Law 47.—An Umpire in only justified in appealing to the other Umpire
when he is unable to decide, owing to his having been prevented from seeing
the occurance on which the appeal is based. He is not to appeal to the
other Umpire in cases on which he could give, a decision, merely because he
is unwilling to give that decision. If he be in any doubt, the principle laid
down in Law 43, “ That the existing state of things shall continue,” shall he
followed, and in consequence, the decision should be in favour of the
batsman.
Law 48.—The special attention of Umpires is called to this Law, which
directs them to call “ No Ball,” unless absolutely satisfied of the fairness of
the delivery.
Umpires should not allow themselves to be unduly influenced by appeals
from such of the field who were not in a position to form a judgment on
the point appealed upon, or by tricks--such as throwing up the ball, on
appealing for a catch at the wicket, without waiting for the decision.
Umpires being the sole judges of fair or unfair play, should remember that
such devices arc obviously unfair, and are not in accordance with the spirit
in which cricket should be played.
Umpires are not justified in deciding the ground unlit for play merely
because the grass is wet and the ball would, in consequence, be slippery.
In order to facilitate play at the earliest possible moment in wet weather
the Umpire shall see that the foot holes made by the bowlers and batsmen
arc cleaned out, dried, and filled up with sawdust at any time during the
match, although the game is not actually in progress.
Umpires should sec that there is no infringement of Law 5. When in
doubt as to the width of a bat they should use the gauge.
All Umpires shall report themselves to the Manager of the Ground half
an hour before the commencement of each day’s play and they shall make a
point of seeing that the wickets, bats and balls are in accordance with the
Laws of the game and they must remember that the conduct of the game
and the state of the ground is within their jurisdiction.
The interval for luncheon shall be 40 minutes. In the event of the last
wicket falling within two minutes of the time arranged for luncheon the
game shall be resumed at the usual hour, no allowance being made for the
ten minutes between the innings.
Umpires shall allow six runs for all hits that drop over and dear of the
boundary line or fence.

RULES OF COUNTY CRBCWET.
1.—A cricketer may not play for more than one County within the
Calendar Year. The penalty for a infringement of this rule to be dis-
qualification for two years. A British Colony, Dependency or State, shall,
for the purpose of this rule, be regarded as a County.
•2.—Qualification by Birth.—A cricketer is always eligible to play
for the county of his birth.
Note.
Although a cricketer can always return to the county of his birth, it is
expedient as an act of courtesy, to give notice to the county for which he
has had a residential qualification if lie intends to leave it.
3.—Qualification by Residence.—A cricketer is qualified to play for
any county in which he has resided for the previous 24 months and is
residing but :—
(a) The mere acquirement or hiring of a tenement, unless used as a
bona fide home, does not constitute “ residence.”
(u) The occupation of a tenement during the cricket season only does
not constitute “residence.”
Note.
A residential qualification cannot be obtained through a School or
University unless such School or University has also been the home.
4.—Where a cricketer uses as residences in the course of the year,
tenements in more than one county, or whore he leaves the country for the
winter months, and in all other cases where his qualification is in any doubt,
it is obligatory on the county for which he wishes to play to prove his
qualification to the satisfaction of the M.U.C.
21
A cricketer who has played for a county for five successive years is
qualified to play for that county for the rest of his cricket career, or until
he plays for some other county.*
Note.
This docs not, include a cricketer who has resided in but has not played
for a county.
6.—A cricketer may play for the county for which he has acquired a
residential qualification, for two successive years immediately after he has
ceased to reside in such county.
7.—Any person on the Active List of the Army or Navy who has played
for a County under the residential qualification for two or more seasons and
is abroad on service shall retain his qualification on his return from abroad
as if he had never been abroad.
8.—Transfers.—A cricketer, already qualified for, or engaged by, a
county but wishing to qualify by residence for another county, must give
notice in writing to the Cricket Chib Committee of the former county before
he commences such residence ; and a County Cricket Chib wishing to engage,
under a residential qualification, a cricketer who is already qualified for.
or engaged by, another County Chib, must inform the committee of the
latter before commencing negotiations with the cricketer.
Note.
This rule includes a cricketer who has never played for a county.
8.—Appeal.—Should any question arise under those rules it shall be
left to the decision of the M.C.C. Committee which shall be final, and in
the event of an infringement of Rule 7, the M.C.C. Committee shall have
special authority to fix the date when the cricketer shall become qualified
by residence or even to permanently bar his so qualifying.
Note.
These rules so far as they are not inconsistent with the Minor Counties
Cricket Association Rules, hereinafter mentioned, apply also to the Minor
Counties.
For purposes of county cricket, county boundaries are not aflected by
the Local Government Act, 1888.
CLASSIFICATION OF COUNTIES.
Cricketing counties shall lie considered as belonging to first-class or
second-class. There is no necessity for further sub-division.
First-class counties are those whose matches, with one another, with
M.C.C. and Ground, with the Universities, with the Australians, and such
other elevens as shall be adjudged “first-class matches” by the M.C.C.
Committee, are used in compilation of first-class batting and howling
averages.
Second-class Counties are those not included in the first-class and second
elevens of first-class counties.
There shall be no limit to the number of first or second-class
counties. The M.C.C. Committee may bring new counties into the list,
may. remove existing ones from it or may do both.
THE COUNTY CHAMPIONSHIP.
FIRST CLASS.
After the close of each cricket season the Committee of the M.C.C. shall
decide the county championship.
It shall be competed for by first-class counties. No county shall be
eligible unless it shall have played at least eight out and home matches
with other counties, provided that if no play can take place owing to
weather or other unavoidable cause such match shall be reckoned as
unfinished.*
To ascertain the position of the Counties in the Championship draws and
losses shall be ignored and only matches won shall count in the percentage
of matches played.
The county which during the season shall have, in finished matches,
obtained the greatest proportionate number of points shall be reckoned
champion county.
"This number has, on several occasions, when the Australians have
visited this Country, been reduced by the M.C.C.
